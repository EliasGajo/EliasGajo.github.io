E2

This chip contains some exfoliated flakes of MoS2. These flakes have been transferred onto a Si + SiO2 substrate without any patterns. Then the aim of this sample (as well as E1, E3, and E4) is to define and isolate the active regions of our 2D material. We will also use the successful active regions to fabricate a plane surface gas sensor.

Composition before patterning

- Substrate Layer 1
Material Si + SiO2 PPA
Thickness SiO2 : 200 nm 100 nm

Substrate preparation

  • Diced part of a wafer of Si + 200 nm SiO2
  • Cleaned in acetone during 5 minutes
  • Cleaned in isopropanole during 5 minutes
  • Dried with a N2 gun

Interesting regions

Here are the interesting regions of this chip (the regions where we transferred very thin layers of MoS2) :

Region 1

Hydrophilic treatment

The surface where the MoS2 has been transferred became hydrophobe (due to the scotch tape) and doesn't allow us to spin coat a PPA layer on it. Then, we apply the following treatment in order to remove this hydrophobicity.

  • Chip deeped in a remover 1165 solution during 30 minutes
  • Chip deeped in a AZ726MIF solution during 30 minutes
  • Chip rinsed in water
  • Chip rinsed in IPA
  • Chip baked at 110°C during 3 minutes for dehydration

PPA spin coating

Role of this layer : Layer to pattern with t-SPL technique.
Steps followed for the fabrication :

  • Spin coated PPA 5% at 2000 rpm (theoretical thickness : 100 nm)
  • Chip baked at 90 degrees during 3 minutes

Total thickness of the layer : 100 nm
Thickness measured : No

PPA patterning

Oxygen plasma RIE

The idea now is to etch the chip with the oxygen plasma solution and to detect the etched thickness with the optical microscope after each try.

Result

Failed ! The first time I put was 30 seconds and it was too much ! All the PPA and most of the MoS2 are gone.

Part 2

There is still some remaining MoS2 on the chip. Thanks to RIE experiment 3 and RIE experiment 4, we know that the maximum etching time (the time to remove all the PPA) is around 2.5 seconds. Then we will use this chip to test the interesting regions isolation (as we have no more interesting regions, we have nothing to loose).

We see no differences at the optical microscope. AFM measurements confirm this observation and we can conclude that we etched almost nothing with this oxygen plasma recipe.

Part 3

We failed again the interesting regions isolation but we still have some MoS2 on the chip. The interesting region that we considered in the part 2 is quite thick and is difficult to etch with an O2 plasma. That is why in the part 3, we will try to isolate the interesting region with another plasma recipe.

PMGI spin coating

The PMGI is not mandatory as the PPA layer will survive to the RIE. However, the surface of the PPA layer will be more flat with a PMGI layer underneath and the patterning will be easier and better.
Steps followed for the fabrication :

  • Chip baked at 190 degrees during 5 minutes (for dehydration)
  • Spin coated PMGI SF2s at 1000 rpm (theoretical thickness : 90 nm)
  • Chip baked at 190 degrees during 3 minutes
  • Let it cool down
  • Spin coated PMGI SF2s at 1000 rpm (theoretical thickness : 90 nm)
  • Chip baked at 190 degrees during 3 minutes

Total thickness of the layer : 180 nm
Thickness measured : No

RIE 3

Now we run the reactive ion etching with the "Si3N4_Smooth" recipe during 10 seconds. This recipe was experimented in sample 1 and is interesting as it seems to etch a lot of different materials without beeing too agressive on the PPA. Indeed, 10 seconds of RIE will only etch around 20 nm of PPA (and 15 nm of SiO2).

After RIE

After lift-off

Now, we see that we achieved to isolate our interesting regions. However, this plasma recipe also etches the SiO2. AFM measurements show that the etched thickness of SiO2 is maximum 10 nm under the contours of our interesting regions. In the future, we can reduce this unwanted effect by reducing the etching time.
